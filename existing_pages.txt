Yules was about to disprove a hundred years of medical advice. </p> <p style="font-size:18px; line-height:1.75; color:#2c2417; margin:0 0 18px 0;"> For most of the twentieth century, doctors prescribed a small glass of brandy or whiskey to patients who couldn't sleep. The reasoning was simple. Alcohol is a sedative. It makes you drowsy. It shortens the time it takes to fall asleep. Therefore, alcohol must help with sleep. </p> <p style="font-size:18px; line-height:1.75; color:#2c2417; margin:0 0 18px 0;"> What no one had done — what no one could do, until the technology existed — was actually watch what happened to the brain during those eight hours after the drink. </p> <p style="font-size:18px; line-height:1.75; color:#2c2417; margin:0 0 18px 0;"> Yules had the technology. By the mid-1960s, the standardized EEG had made it possible to record the electrical activity of a sleeping brain across an entire night and identify exactly which sleep stage was occurring at any moment. Eugene Aserinsky had discovered REM sleep just thirteen years earlier (Issue 005). The full architecture of sleep — the cycles, the stages, the rhythms — was being mapped for the first time. </p> <p style="font-size:18px; line-height:1.75; color:#2c2417; margin:0 0 18px 0;"> So Yules, working in the psychiatry department, decided to ask a question that had never been answered scientifically. What does alcohol actually do to the brain during sleep? </p> <p style="font-size:18px; line-height:1.75; color:#2c2417; margin:0 0 18px 0;"> He recruited three young men. He attached electrodes to their scalps. He gave each of them one gram of ethanol per kilogram of body weight — roughly the equivalent of two to three stiff drinks — fifteen minutes before bedtime. He recorded their brain activity through the entire night. Then he did it again the next night. And the next. For five consecutive nights. </p> <p style="font-size:18px; line-height:1.75; color:#2c2417; margin:0 0 28px 0;"> What the printouts showed was the opposite of what every doctor had been telling patients. </p> <hr style="border:none; border-top:1px solid #e8dcc8; margin:0 0 28px 0;"> <h2 style="font-size:22px; color:#2c2417; margin:0 0 18px 0; font-family:Georgia, serif;"> The two halves of an alcohol night </h2> <p style="font-size:18px; line-height:1.75; color:#2c2417; margin:0 0 18px 0;"> The first half of the night looked, on the surface, exactly like what people expected. The men fell asleep faster. The early-night EEG showed deep, slow-wave activity — the kind of deep sleep we covered in Issue 005. By every superficial measure, the alcohol appeared to be working as a sleep aid. </p> <p style="font-size:18px; line-height:1.75; color:#2c2417; margin:0 0 18px 0;"> But something was missing. </p> <p style="font-size:18px; line-height:1.75; color:#2c2417; margin:0 0 18px 0;"> REM sleep — the dreaming phase, the emotional processing phase, the <strong>overnight therapy</strong> we covered in Issue 013 — was suppressed. The first REM cycle, which normally arrives around 90 minutes after sleep onset, was delayed. When it finally appeared, it was shorter than baseline. The brain was being pushed into deep slow-wave sleep at the expense of the REM cycles that should have followed. </p> <p style="font-size:18px; line-height:1.75; color:#2c2417; margin:0 0 18px 0;"> Then, around the three-and-a-half hour mark, the second half of the night began. And everything fell apart. </p> <p style="font-size:22px; line-height:1.5; color:#2c2417; margin:30px 0; text-align:center; font-style:italic;"> The brain, having been chemically suppressed in REM during the first half, surged back with fragmented, restless REM in the second half. </p> <p style="font-size:18px; line-height:1.75; color:#2c2417; margin:0 0 28px 0;"> As the alcohol metabolized — and the liver clears alcohol at a roughly linear rate, about one standard drink per hour — the sedative effect disappeared. What replaced it was a rebound. The men woke up. They tossed. They moved. Their EEGs showed repeated micro-arousals. Their heart rates climbed. Their sleep, in the hours when emotional processing and memory consolidation should have been peaking, was being broken apart by their own bodies trying to recover the REM they had been denied. </p> <p style="font-size:18px; line-height:1.75; color:#2c2417; margin:0 0 28px 0;"> Yules published his findings in 1966. Two years later, Knowles, Laverty, and Kuechler confirmed and extended the results. By the early 1970s, the picture was unmistakable. The nightcap had been wrong for a century. </p> <hr style="border:none; border-top:1px solid #e8dcc8; margin:0 0 28px 0;"> <h2 style="font-size:22px; color:#2c2417; margin:0 0 18px 0; font-family:Georgia, serif;"> What is actually happening in your brain </h2> <p style="font-size:18px; line-height:1.75; color:#2c2417; margin:0 0 18px 0;"> The mechanism that Yules observed but couldn't fully explain has since been mapped at the molecular level. </p> <p style="font-size:18px; line-height:1.75; color:#2c2417; margin:0 0 18px 0;"> Alcohol initially binds to <strong>GABA receptors</strong> — the same receptors that prescription sleeping pills target. GABA is the brain's primary inhibitory neurotransmitter. When you drink, GABA signaling is amplified, neurons fire less, and the brain quiets. This produces the sedation you feel as drowsiness. </p> <p style="font-size:18px; line-height:1.75; color:#2c2417; margin:0 0 18px 0;"> But as the alcohol metabolizes, the brain compensates. <strong>Glutamate</strong> — the brain's primary excitatory neurotransmitter — surges back. This is called the <em>glutamatergic rebound</em>. It is the chemical signature of the second half of an alcohol night. Your brain, having been over-suppressed for several hours, now fires excessively. You wake up. Your heart pounds. Your mind races. You cannot fall back asleep. </p> <p style="font-size:18px; line-height:1.75; color:#2c2417; margin:0 0 28px 0;"> A 2013 review by Irshaad Ebrahim and colleagues at the London Sleep Centre analyzed all known studies of alcohol's effects on sleep in healthy adults. At every dose tested — including low doses of roughly two standard drinks — REM sleep was significantly delayed and total nighttime REM was reduced. The higher the dose, the worse the disruption. There is no dose at which alcohol is neutral for sleep. There is no glass of wine that produces restorative rest. </p> <hr style="border:none; border-top:1px solid #e8dcc8; margin:0 0 28px 0;"> <h2 style="font-size:22px; color:#2c2417; margin:0 0 18px 0; font-family:Georgia, serif;"> Why this matters more after 50 </h2> <p style="font-size:18px; line-height:1.75; color:#2c2417; margin:0 0 18px 0;"> Three things compound to make alcohol's effect on sleep significantly worse after age 50. </p> <p style="font-size:18px; line-height:1.75; color:#2c2417; margin:0 0 18px 0;"> First, <strong>liver function declines with age</strong>. The enzymes that metabolize alcohol — primarily alcohol dehydrogenase — become less efficient. The same two glasses of wine that cleared by 2am at age 35 may still be in your system at 5am at age 60. The window of REM suppression extends. The rebound arrives later in the night, often during the hours when REM should be most concentrated. </p> <p style="font-size:18px; line-height:1.75; color:#2c2417; margin:0 0 18px 0;"> Second, <strong>REM sleep already declines naturally with age</strong>. By your fifties and sixties, your total nightly REM is significantly less than it was at 25. There is less REM to lose. And alcohol cuts into a window that is already smaller — which means the percentage of REM affected is disproportionately larger. </p> <p style="font-size:18px; line-height:1.75; color:#2c2417; margin:0 0 18px 0;"> Third, <strong>perimenopause and menopause introduce their own sleep disruptions</strong> through hormonal changes. Alcohol compounds these effects, intensifying nighttime awakenings and hot flashes for many women. </p> <p style="font-size:22px; line-height:1.5; color:#2c2417; margin:30px 0; text-align:center; font-style:italic;"> "I had two glasses of wine with dinner and woke at 3am" is a near-universal experience after 50. It is not bad luck. It is the predictable consequence of chemistry. </p> <p style="font-size:24px; color:#8b7355; margin:30px 0; text-align:center; letter-spacing:12px;">· · ·</p> <div style="border:1px solid #e8dcc8; border-radius:6px; padding:30px 28px; margin:0 0 35px 0; background-color:#fffdf7;"> <h2 style="font-size:22px; color:#2c2417; margin:0 0 18px 0; font-family:Georgia, serif;"> The One Thing Worth Trying This Week </h2> <p style="font-size:18px; line-height:1.75; color:#2c2417; margin:0 0 18px 0;"> You do not have to stop drinking. This is not about quitting alcohol. </p> <p style="font-size:18px; line-height:1.75; color:#2c2417; margin:0 0 18px 0;"> The single most useful experiment you can run this week is to test the timing. If you drink in the evening, try stopping at least three hours before your intended sleep time. Not three minutes. Not thirty. <strong>Three hours.</strong> A standard drink metabolizes at roughly one per hour, so this window gives your liver time to clear it before sleep begins. </p> <p style="font-size:18px; line-height:1.75; color:#2c2417; margin:0 0 18px 0;"> Or run the cleaner version of the experiment. Pick one week. Drink nothing after 5pm. Pay attention to the second half of your sleep — particularly the hours between 3am and 6am — and notice what changes. Most people report the same thing: deeper sleep in the second half, fewer 3am wake-ups, and waking up feeling like something has been resolved overnight instead of carried forward. </p> <p style="font-size:18px; line-height:1.75; color:#2c2417; margin:0 0 0 0;"> This is not about virtue. It is about the chemistry Yules documented in 1966 and that every subsequent study has confirmed. Your body has been telling you something about the second half of your night. — <em>The Hive Mind</em> </p> </div> <h2 style="font-size:22px; color:#2c2417; margin:0 0 18px 0; font-family:Georgia, serif;"> Until next issue </h2> <p style="font-size:18px; line-height:1.75; color:#2c2417; margin:0 0 28px 0;"> Next: the half-life of your morning coffee is longer than you think — and the chemical it's been blocking has been building in your brain since 6am to put you to sleep tonight. </p> <hr style="border:none; border-top:2px solid #d4a847; margin:0 0 0 0;"> <div style="background: linear-gradient(135deg, #8b4513, #a0522d, #6b3410); padding:40px 30px; border-radius:8px; margin:35px 0; text-align:center;"> <h2 style="font-size:24px; color:#fffdf7; margin:0 0 15px 0; font-family:Georgia, serif; font-weight:bold; font-style:italic;"> Built to Support Your Body's Natural Rhythm </h2> <p style="font-size:18px; line-height:1.65; color:#fffdf7; margin:0 0 25px 0; font-family:Georgia, serif; opacity:0.9;"> Beezy Beez Botanical Extract Sleep Honey is designed to support the wind-down phase of your circadian cycle — when your body wants to drop into rest, but stress or overstimulation gets in the way. color:#8b7355; margin:0 0 10px 0;"> The Hive Mind · Issue 013 · 5 min read </p> <h1 style="font-size:32px; font-weight:bold; color:#2c2417; margin:0 0 12px 0; line-height:1.25; font-family:Georgia, serif;"> Your Brain Runs Therapy While You Sleep — Forgetting the Dream Is How You Know It Worked </h1> <p style="font-size:20px; color:#5a4a3a; margin:0 0 30px 0; line-height:1.5; font-style:italic;"> A French neuroscientist, a cat that hunted invisible prey in its sleep, and the overnight therapy session your brain runs every night without asking. </p> <img src="https://cdn.shopify.com/s/files/1/0616/0616/6777/files/The_Hive_Mind_Issue_13.png?v=1778537395" width="100%" style="display:block; width:100%; height:auto; margin:0 0 35px 0; border-radius:4px;" alt="The Hive Mind Issue 013 — Dreams and Emotional Processing"> <p style="font-size:18px; line-height:1.75; color:#2c2417; margin:0 0 18px 0;"> In 1959, a French neuroscientist named Michel Jouvet was watching a sleeping cat do something impossible. </p> <p style="font-size:18px; line-height:1.75; color:#2c2417; margin:0 0 18px 0;"> Jouvet was working at the University of Lyon, studying the electrical activity of the brain during sleep. He had made a small, precise lesion in a structure called the pons — a region deep in the brainstem — of a cat that was otherwise healthy and neurologically intact. </p> <p style="font-size:18px; line-height:1.75; color:#2c2417; margin:0 0 18px 0;"> The cat fell asleep normally. It entered slow-wave sleep. Then it transitioned into REM — the phase that Aserinsky had discovered just six years earlier, the phase we covered in Issue 005. </p> <p style="font-size:18px; line-height:1.75; color:#2c2417; margin:0 0 18px 0;"> And then the cat stood up. </p> <p style="font-size:18px; line-height:1.75; color:#2c2417; margin:0 0 18px 0;"> Its eyes were closed. Its brain was producing the electrical patterns of deep REM sleep. Every instrument confirmed: this animal was asleep. But it was standing, walking, stalking — crouching as if hunting invisible prey, arching its back as if confronting a threat that wasn't there, sometimes pouncing at nothing. </p> <p style="font-size:18px; line-height:1.75; color:#2c2417; margin:0 0 18px 0;"> The cat was acting out its dreams. </p> <p style="font-size:18px; line-height:1.75; color:#2c2417; margin:0 0 18px 0;"> Jouvet had removed the one brain structure responsible for the paralysis that normally accompanies REM sleep — a phenomenon called <strong>muscle atonia</strong>. In an intact brain, every time you enter REM, your body is rendered almost completely immobile from the neck down. Not because something is wrong. Because your brain is doing something so vivid, so physically real in its neural representation, that if your muscles were not disabled, you would move. You would thrash. You would fight the thing in the dream. </p> <p style="font-size:18px; line-height:1.75; color:#2c2417; margin:0 0 18px 0;"> The paralysis is not a side effect. It is a safety mechanism. </p> <p style="font-size:18px; line-height:1.75; color:#2c2417; margin:0 0 18px 0;"> Jouvet called this state <strong>"paradoxical sleep"</strong> — paradoxical because the brain was intensely active while the body was completely still. He spent the next four decades studying it. He kept a personal dream diary for over 35 years. He founded what he called the Laboratory of Molecular Dream Science — a name he chose, he said, as a quiet protest against a scientific establishment that didn't take dreams seriously. </p> <p style="font-size:18px; line-height:1.75; color:#2c2417; margin:0 0 28px 0;"> What he couldn't answer, and what haunted him until his death in 2017, was the question that matters most for your sleep tonight: <em>why?</em> What is the brain actually doing during those vivid, emotionally charged sequences — and why does it go to such extraordinary lengths to run them? </p> <hr style="border:none; border-top:1px solid #e8dcc8; margin:0 0 28px 0;"> <h2 style="font-size:22px; color:#2c2417; margin:0 0 18px 0; font-family:Georgia, serif;"> The overnight therapy your brain runs without asking </h2> <p style="font-size:18px; line-height:1.75; color:#2c2417; margin:0 0 18px 0;"> In 2011, a neuroscientist at UC Berkeley named Matthew Walker published a study that began to answer Jouvet's question. </p> <p style="font-size:18px; line-height:1.75; color:#2c2417; margin:0 0 18px 0;"> Walker showed participants emotionally provocative images and measured their amygdala response using fMRI. Then he divided them into two groups. One slept normally. The other stayed awake. Twelve hours later, both groups saw the same images again. </p> <p style="font-size:18px; line-height:1.75; color:#2c2417; margin:0 0 18px 0;"> The group that slept showed a <strong>significant reduction in emotional reactivity</strong>. The images still registered — they hadn't forgotten them — but the sharp edge was gone. The group that stayed awake showed no such reduction. </p> <p style="font-size:18px; line-height:1.75; color:#2c2417; margin:0 0 18px 0;"> The degree of emotional resolution correlated specifically with the amount of REM sleep each participant got — and with a specific neurochemical condition that only exists during REM. </p> <p style="font-size:22px; line-height:1.5; color:#2c2417; margin:30px 0; text-align:center; font-style:italic;"> During REM sleep, the brain is completely devoid of norepinephrine — the brain's primary stress and anxiety chemical. </p> <p style="font-size:18px; line-height:1.75; color:#2c2417; margin:0 0 18px 0;"> Norepinephrine is the molecule that creates the racing heart, the sense that something is wrong. It floods the brain during waking stress. It surges during the cortisol spikes we covered in Issue 002. And during REM — and only during REM — it shuts off entirely. </p> <p style="font-size:18px; line-height:1.75; color:#2c2417; margin:0 0 28px 0;"> This means that when your brain replays an emotional memory during a dream, it replays it in the only neurochemical environment in which the memory can be reprocessed without triggering the stress response. The memory is reactivated. The emotional centers fire. But the anxiety chemical is absent. Walker called it <strong>overnight therapy</strong>. </p> <hr style="border:none; border-top:1px solid #e8dcc8; margin:0 0 28px 0;"> <h2 style="font-size:22px; color:#2c2417; margin:0 0 18px 0; font-family:Georgia, serif;"> Why forgetting the dream might be the point </h2> <p style="font-size:18px; line-height:1.75; color:#2c2417; margin:0 0 18px 0;"> Most people believe that the value of a dream is in its content — that if you could just remember it, decode its symbols, you would learn something about yourself. Dream dictionaries. Dream journals. Dream analysis. </p> <p style="font-size:18px; line-height:1.75; color:#2c2417; margin:0 0 18px 0;"> The research suggests something almost opposite. </p> <p style="font-size:18px; line-height:1.75; color:#2c2417; margin:0 0 18px 0;"> The therapeutic function of dreaming does not require you to remember the dream.
